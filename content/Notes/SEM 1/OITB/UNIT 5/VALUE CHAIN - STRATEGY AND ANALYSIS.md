## 1. VALUE CHAIN

1. *<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">M. Porter</mark> introduced the generic value chain model in <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">1985</mark>.
2. Value chain <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">represents all the internal activities a firm engages in to produce goods and services.</mark>
3. VC is formed of
	1. <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">primary activities</mark> that <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">add value to the final product directly</mark>
	2. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">support activities</mark> that add value <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">indirectly</mark>.

## 2. PORTERS VALUE CHAIN MODEL

### Primary activities:
are those that go directly into the creation of a product or the execution of a service, including:

#### 1. Inbound logistics:
Activities related to receiving, warehousing, and inventory management of source materials and components
#### 2. Operations:
Activities related to turning raw materials and components into a finished product
#### 3. Outbound logistics:
Activities related to distribution, including packaging, sorting, and shipping
#### 4. Marketing and sales:
Activities related to the marketing and sale of a product or service, including promotion, advertising, and pricing strategy
#### 5. After-sales services:
Activities that take place after a sale has been finalized, including installation, training, quality assurance, repair, and customer service

### Secondary activities:
help primary activities become more efficient—effectively creating a competitive advantage—and are broken down into:
#### 1. Procurement:
Activities related to the sourcing of raw materials, components, equipment, and services
#### 2. Technological development:
Activities related to research and development, including product design, market research, and process development
#### 3. Human resources management:
Activities related to the recruitment, hiring, training, development, retention, and compensation of employees
#### 4. Infrastructure:
Activities related to the company’s .overhead and management, including financing and planning.

## 3. VALUE CHAIN - STRATEGIC MANAGEMENT

1. The value chain concept is based on the <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">process view of organizations</mark>.
2. It is an idea of<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;"> considering a</mark><mark style="background: #CACFD9A6;"> manufacturing (or service) </mark><mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">organization as a dynamic system.</mark>
3. A Dynamic system made up of various <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">subsystems</mark>, <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">each with inputs, transformation processes and outputs.</mark>
4. The inputs, transformations, and outputs require the<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> acquisition and consumption of company resources,</mark> such as money, equipment, materials, labor, buildings, land, administration and management.
5. The management process of carrying out value chain activities <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">determines the costs and affects the profitability of organizations.</mark>
6. Most organizations <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">engage in hundreds of activities</mark> while converting its inputs to outputs.
7. These activities are classified as either<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> primary or support activities</mark>

## 4. VALUE CHAIN ANALYSIS

*DEFINITION*
  1. A process when an <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">organization identifies its primary and support activities that add value to its final product </mark>
  2. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">then analyze these activities to reduce costs or increase differentiation.</mark>

*UNDERSTANDING THE TOOL*

1. Value chain analysis is a strategy tool used to <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">analyze internal firm activities</mark>.
2. Its goal is to recognize, which activities are the <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">most valuable</mark> (i.e. <mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">provide</mark>, <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">cost or differentiation advantage</mark>) to the firm and <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">which ones could be improved to provide competitive advantage</mark>.
3. By looking into internal activities, the <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">analysis reveals where a firm’s competitive advantages or disadvantages are.</mark> 
4. The<mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;"> firm that competes through differentiation advantage will</mark> <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">try to perform its activities better than competitors</mark> .
5. <mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;">If it competes through cost advantage</mark>, it will <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">try to perform internal activities at lower costs than competitors</mark> .
6. Thus, When a company is capable of <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">producing goods at lower costs than the market price or to provide superior products, it earns profits.</mark>
7. Value chain analysis is a means of evaluating each of the activities in a company’s value chain to understand where opportunities for improvement lie.
8. Conducting a value chain analysis prompts you to <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">consider how each step adds or subtracts value from your final product or service.</mark>
9. This, in turn, can help you <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">realize some form of competitive advantage</mark>, such as:
	1. **Cost reduction**, by <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">making each activity</mark> in the value chain <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">more efficient and, therefore, less expensive.</mark>
	2. **Product differentiation**, by i<mark style="background: #CACFD9A6;">nvesting more time and resources into</mark> activities like <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">research and development, design, or marketing that can help your product stand out</mark>

10. Typically, <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">increasing the performance of one of the four secondary activities can benefit at least one of the primary activities.</mark>
### HOW TO CONDUCT VALUE CHAIN ANALYSIS

1. Identify Value Chain Activities
	1. <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">understand all</mark> of the primary and secondary <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">activities that go into your product or service’s creation.</mark>
	2. If your company sells <mark style="background: #CACFD9A6;">multiple products</mark> or services, it’s important to perform this <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">process for each one.</mark>

<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">2. Determine the Cost and Value of Activities
</mark>:
1. Once the primary and secondary activities have been identified, the next step is to <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">determine the value that each activity adds to the process, along with the costs involved.</mark>
2. When <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">thinking about the value created by activities</mark>, ask yourself:
	1. How does each increase the <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">end user’s satisfaction or enjoyment</mark>?
	2. How does it <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">create value for my firm</mark>?
	3. For example, does constructing the product out of <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">certain materials make it more durable or luxurious</mark> for the user?
	4. Does including a <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">certain feature make it more likely your firm will benefit from network effects and increased business</mark>?
3. Similarly, it’s important to <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">understand the costs associated with each step</mark> in the process. Depending on your situation, you may find that lowering expenses is an easy way to improve the value each transaction provides.

**<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">3. Identify Opportunities for Competitive Advantage</mark>**
1. Once you’ve compiled your value chain and understand the cost and value associated with each step, you can analyze it through the lens of whatever <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">competitive advantage</mark> you’re trying to achieve.
2. For example, if your<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> primary goal is to reduce your firm’s costs, you should evaluate each piece of your value chain through the lens of reducing expenses.</mark> 
	1. <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">Which steps could be more efficient?</mark>
	2. Are there any that <mark style="background: #BBFABBA6;">don’t create significant value and could be outsourced or eliminated to substantially reduce costs</mark>?
3. Similarly, if your primary goal is to achieve <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">product differentiation</mark>,
	1. which parts of your value chain offer the best opportunity to realize that goal?
	2. <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">Would the value created justify the investment of additional resource</mark>s?
