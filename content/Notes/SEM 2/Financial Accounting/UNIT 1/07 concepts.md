## WHAT ARE ACCOUNTING CONCEPTS?

 These are <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">basic rules, assumptions and principles</mark> which <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">act as primary standard</mark> for r<mark style="background: #ABF7F7A6;">ecording business transactions and maintaining uniformity in books of accounts.</mark>

## Objectives of Accounting concepts

1. Maintain <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">UNIFORMITY and REGULARITY</mark> in preparation of <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">account statements</mark>
2. Principles that help the accountant in<mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;"> preparation and maintenance of business records</mark>
3. <mark style="background: #FFF3A3A6;">Understand business rules and regulations</mark> required to be followed by all types of entities

### 1. Business Entity Concept

 Definition:
1. The business entity concept states that the <mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;">business and its owner(s) are separate entities for accounting purposes</mark>. 
2. Financial <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">transactions of the business are recorded separately from those of the owners.</mark>

 Importance:
- Ensures clarity in financial records by separating personal and business finances.
- Enhances the accuracy of financial statements.

 Example:
If an <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">owner invests personal money into the business, it is recorded as a capital contribution to the business</mark>. Similarly, any <mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;">withdrawals made by the owner for personal use are recorded as drawings.</mark>

### 2. Money Measurement Concept

 Definition:
According to the money measurement concept, <mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;">only those transactions and events that can be measured in monetary terms are recorded</mark> in the accounting books.

 Importance:
- Provides a uniform measurement criterion for financial reporting.
- Ensures that financial statements reflect quantifiable data.

 Example:
<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">A company’s goodwill or the efficiency of its employees is not recorded in the financial statements because these cannot be measured in monetary terms. </mark>However, the purchase of equipment for Rs 10,000 is recorded because it has a definite monetary value.

### 3. Going Concern Concept / continuity

 Definition:
The going concern concept <mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;">assumes that a business will continue its operations for the foreseeable future</mark> and<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;"> has no intention or necessity to liquidate </mark>or significantly scale down .

 Importance:
- Provides a basis for valuing assets and liabilities.
- Influences the application of accounting policies.

 Example:
<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">Assets are recorded at cost rather than liquidation value, as the business is expected to use them over time.</mark> For instance, a building purchased for Rs 200,000 is recorded at purchase price, not at a potential selling price of Rs 250,000.

### 4. Cost Concept

 Definition:
<mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;">The cost concept states that assets are recorded at their the purchase price and not at their current market value.</mark>

 Importance:
- Ensures objectivity and reliability of financial records.
- Provides a consistent basis for asset valuation.

 Example:
<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">If a company buys machinery for Rs 50,000, it is recorded at this purchase price, even if its market value changes over time.</mark>

### 5. Realization Concept

 Definition:
The realization concept dictates that <mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;">revenue should be recognized and recorded when it is earned, regardless of when the cash is received.</mark>

 Importance:
- Ensures that income is matched with the period in which it is earned.
- Reflects the true performance of a business.

 Example:
If a company <mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">provides services worth Rs 5,000 in December</mark> but <mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;">receives payment in January</mark>, the <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">revenue is recorded in December when the services were rendered</mark>.

### 6. Matching Concept

 Definition:
The matching concept requires that <mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">expenses be matched with the revenues they help to generate in the same accounting period.</mark>

 Importance:
- Ensures accurate measurement of profit for a specific period.
- Provides a clear picture of business performance.

 Example:
If a company incurs Rs 2,000 in advertising expenses in December to generate sales for that month, the expense is recorded in December to match it with the related revenue.

### 7. Accrual Concept

 Definition:
<mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;">The accrual concept states that transactions are recorded when they occur, not when cash is received or paid. This includes recognizing revenues when earned and expenses when incurred.</mark>

 Importance:
- Provides a more accurate financial position of the business.
- Reflects all financial activities within a period.

 Example:
<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">Interest expense of Rs 1,000 that has accrued but not yet been paid by the end of the year is recorded as an expense for that year, even though the payment will be made in the next year.</mark>

### 8. Conservatism (Prudence) Concept

 Definition:
The conservatism concept advises that<mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;"> potential expenses and liabilities should be recognized as soon as possible,</mark> but <mark style="background: #FF5582A6;">revenues should only be recognized when they are assured of being received.</mark>

 Importance:
- Prevents overstatement of assets and income.
- Ensures cautious and realistic financial reporting.

 Example:
<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">If a company expects a bad debt of Rs 3,000, it should record it as an expense immediately. However, potential income from a lawsuit should only be recorded when it is virtually certain to be wo</mark>n.

### 9. Consistency Concept

 Definition:
The consistency concept requires that the <mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;">same accounting methods and principles be used from period to period, allowing for comparability over time.</mark>

 Importance:
- Facilitates comparison of financial statements over different periods.
- Enhances reliability of financial information.

 Example:
<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">If a company uses the straight line depreciation method for inventory valuation, it should continue using this method</mark> in future periods unless a justified change is made and disclosed.

### 10. Materiality Concept

 Definition:
The materiality concept states that<mark style="background: #FFB86CA6;"> all significant items, i.e., items that could influence the decision-making of users, should be disclosed in the financial statements.</mark>

 Importance:
- Ensures that financial statements are useful to users.
- Focuses on significant information, avoiding immaterial details.

 Example:
<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">A company with total assets of Rs 5 million may not need to separately disclose a Rs 100 expense, but a Rs 50,000 expense would be considered material and should be disclosed.</mark>

### 11. Full Disclosure

#### Definition:
1. The full disclosure convention requires that <mark style="background: #D2B3FFA6;">all material information, both financial and non-financial, should be disclosed in the financial statements</mark> or notes to the financial statements. 
2. This information should be sufficient to allow users to make informed decisions.

#### Importance:
- **Transparency**: Full disclosure ensures that all relevant information is available to stakeholders, enhancing the transparency of financial statements.
- **Informed Decisions**: Users can make better-informed decisions when they have access to all pertinent information.
- **Compliance**: It helps in complying with legal and regulatory requirements.

#### Example:
<mark style="background: #FFB8EBA6;">Significant accounting policies, contingent liabilities, subsequent events, and any changes in accounting methods should be disclosed</mark>. For instance, if a company changes its inventory valuation method from FIFO to LIFO, it must disclose this change and its impact on the financial statements.
