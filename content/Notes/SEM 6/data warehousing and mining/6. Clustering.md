# 6. Clustering

Clustering is one of the most important unsupervised learning techniques in Data Mining. It is used to group similar data objects into clusters so that objects within the same cluster are highly similar, while objects belonging to different clusters are dissimilar.

Unlike classification:

- Clustering does not require predefined class labels.
    
- Groups are formed automatically based on similarities in data.
    

Clustering is widely used in:

- Market analysis
    
- Pattern recognition
    
- Image processing
    
- Medical diagnosis
    
- Customer segmentation
    
- Social network analysis
    

---

# 6.1 Introduction to Clustering

## 1. Definition of Clustering

Clustering is the process of grouping a set of data objects into clusters such that:

- Objects within the same cluster are similar
    
- Objects in different clusters are dissimilar
    

Each cluster represents a collection of related data objects.

---

## 2. Example of Clustering

Suppose a shopping website groups customers based on:

- Purchasing habits
    
- Age
    
- Income
    
- Interests
    

Customers with similar behavior are placed in the same cluster.

---

## 3. Characteristics of Clustering

### 1. Unsupervised Learning Technique

Clustering does not require labeled training data.

The system automatically identifies patterns.

---

### 2. Similarity-Based Grouping

Objects are grouped according to similarity measures.

### Similarity May Be Based On

- Distance
    
- Density
    
- Statistical properties
    

---

### 3. Intra-Cluster Similarity

Objects inside the same cluster should be highly similar.

---

### 4. Inter-Cluster Dissimilarity

Objects belonging to different clusters should be highly different.

---

### 5. Automatic Pattern Discovery

Clustering automatically identifies hidden structures and patterns in data.

---

### 6. Data Reduction

Large datasets can be represented using clusters, simplifying analysis.

---

## 4. Importance of Clustering

Clustering helps:

- Discover hidden patterns
    
- Organize large datasets
    
- Improve decision-making
    
- Support data analysis
    

---

# 6.4 Categorization of Major Clustering Methods

Clustering methods are classified based on how clusters are formed.

Major clustering methods include:

- Partitioning methods
    
- Hierarchical methods
    
- Density-based methods
    
- Grid-based methods
    
- Model-based methods
    

---

# 1. Partitioning Methods

## Definition

Partitioning methods divide data into a predefined number of clusters.

Each object belongs to exactly one cluster.

---

## Characteristics

- Fast and simple
    
- Suitable for medium-sized datasets
    
- Requires number of clusters in advance
    

---

## Examples

- K-Means
    
- K-Medoids
    

---

# 2. Hierarchical Methods

## Definition

Hierarchical clustering creates a hierarchy of clusters.

Clusters are formed either by:

- Merging smaller clusters
    
- Dividing larger clusters
    

---

## Types of Hierarchical Clustering

### 1. Agglomerative Method

Bottom-up approach.

Small clusters merge into larger clusters.

---

### 2. Divisive Method

Top-down approach.

Large clusters divide into smaller clusters.

---

## Characteristics

- Produces tree-like structure called dendrogram
    
- No need to specify cluster count initially
    

---

## Limitations

- Computationally expensive
    
- Difficult for very large datasets
    

---

# 3. Density-Based Methods

## Definition

Density-based methods form clusters based on dense regions of data points.

Sparse regions separate clusters.

---

## Example

- DBSCAN
    

---

## Characteristics

- Detects arbitrary-shaped clusters
    
- Handles noise effectively
    

---

## Advantages

- Works well for spatial data
    
- Identifies outliers
    

---

# 4. Grid-Based Methods

## Definition

Grid-based methods divide the data space into finite grid cells.

Clustering operations are performed on grids instead of individual objects.

---

## Characteristics

- Fast processing
    
- Efficient for large datasets
    

---

## Advantages

- Computationally efficient
    
- Independent of number of data objects
    

---

# 5. Model-Based Methods

## Definition

Model-based methods assume that data is generated from a statistical model.

Clusters are identified based on probability distributions.

---

## Characteristics

- Uses mathematical models
    
- Handles complex datasets
    

---

## Advantages

- High flexibility
    
- Good clustering accuracy
    

---

## Limitations

- Computational complexity
    
- Requires statistical assumptions
    

---

# 6.6 Partitioning Methods

Partitioning methods divide data into a fixed number of clusters.

The most common partitioning methods are:

- K-Means
    
- K-Medoids
    

---

# K-Means Method

## 1. Definition of K-Means

K-Means is a partitioning clustering algorithm that divides data into K clusters based on mean values.

Each cluster is represented by:

- A centroid (mean point)
    

---

## 2. Objective of K-Means

The objective is to minimize:

- Distance between data points and cluster centroids
    

---

## 3. Working Procedure of K-Means

The K-Means algorithm works iteratively.

---

## Steps of K-Means Algorithm

### Step 1: Select Number of Clusters

Choose value of K.

---

### Step 2: Initialize Centroids

Randomly select K centroids.

---

### Step 3: Assign Data Points

Assign each object to nearest centroid.

---

### Step 4: Recalculate Centroids

Compute new centroid for each cluster.

---

### Step 5: Repeat

Repeat assignment and centroid calculation until clusters stabilize.

---

## 4. Example of K-Means

Suppose customers are grouped into:

- 3 clusters
    

based on:

- Income
    
- Spending behavior
    

The algorithm repeatedly adjusts cluster centers until optimal grouping is achieved.

---

## 5. Advantages of K-Means

### 1. Simple and Easy to Implement

Widely used due to simplicity.

---

### 2. Fast Processing

Efficient for large datasets.

---

### 3. Scalable

Works well for moderate-to-large datasets.

---

### 4. Efficient for Numerical Data

Performs well on continuous variables.

---

## 6. Limitations of K-Means

### 1. Requires Value of K in Advance

User must specify number of clusters.

---

### 2. Sensitive to Initial Centroids

Different starting points may produce different results.

---

### 3. Sensitive to Noise and Outliers

Outliers affect centroid calculation.

---

### 4. Difficulty with Non-Spherical Clusters

Works best for spherical clusters only.

---

# K-Medoids Method

## 1. Definition of K-Medoids

K-Medoids is a partitioning clustering algorithm similar to K-Means, but clusters are represented by actual data objects called medoids instead of mean values.

---

## 2. Medoid

A medoid is the most centrally located object in a cluster.

Unlike centroids:

- Medoids are actual existing data points.
    

---

## 3. Working Procedure of K-Medoids

---

## Steps of K-Medoids Algorithm

### Step 1: Select K Objects as Initial Medoids

Randomly choose medoids.

---

### Step 2: Assign Objects to Nearest Medoid

Objects are grouped based on distance.

---

### Step 3: Replace Medoids

Test swapping medoids with non-medoid objects.

---

### Step 4: Compute Total Cost

Calculate clustering cost.

---

### Step 5: Repeat Until Stable

Continue until no improvement occurs.

---

## 4. Advantages of K-Medoids

### 1. Robust Against Noise

Less affected by outliers.

---

### 2. Uses Actual Data Objects

Medoids are real data points.

---

### 3. Better for Noisy Data

Produces more stable clusters.

---

## 5. Limitations of K-Medoids

### 1. Higher Computational Cost

More expensive than K-Means.

---

### 2. Slower Processing

Not suitable for extremely large datasets.

---

### 3. Requires Number of Clusters

Value of K must be specified.

---
